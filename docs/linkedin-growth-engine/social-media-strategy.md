# Social media goals and content pillars

This document defines the starting social media strategy for the LinkedIn Growth Engine. LinkedIn is the primary channel. Other channels can reuse the best-performing ideas, but the operation should optimize first for LinkedIn conversations, credibility, and qualified product interest.

## Operating premise

The product is for people who make hard calls with too much context and too little clean signal. The social media operation should make that pain visible, show practical ways to work through it, and prove that AI advisors can help people think better without turning every decision into noise.

## Core goals

1. Build trust with decision-heavy professionals.
   - Publish useful thinking before asking for attention.
   - Show judgment, tradeoffs, and examples instead of generic AI claims.
   - Make the brand feel sharp, calm, and useful.

2. Create qualified demand for the product.
   - Turn posts into conversations with founders, operators, consultants, and builders who already feel the decision overload problem.
   - Drive readers toward profile visits, product exploration, signups, demos, or waitlist actions.
   - Use direct replies and comments to qualify interest before selling.

3. Learn which messages convert.
   - Test hooks, examples, objections, and use cases in public.
   - Capture which posts produce saves, comments, replies, profile visits, and product actions.
   - Feed proven language back into landing pages, onboarding, and product copy.

4. Build a repeatable content system.
   - Maintain a small set of content pillars with clear jobs.
   - Turn product work, customer conversations, and founder lessons into posts.
   - Review performance weekly so the operation compounds instead of restarting from scratch.

## Audience segments

### Founder or operator with too many open loops

These readers own strategy, hiring, product, sales, fundraising, or partnerships. They do not need more advice. They need a cleaner way to decide what matters today.

- Pain: competing priorities, half-formed options, and expensive indecision.
- What earns attention: decision frameworks, examples of tradeoffs, and short stories about choosing under uncertainty.
- Desired action: comment with their own decision problem, save the framework, or try the product for a live decision.

### AI-curious builder

These readers are testing agents, AI workflows, and new tools. They want proof that the product does more than wrap a chat box.

- Pain: many AI tools sound useful but collapse when the work needs context, structure, or follow-through.
- What earns attention: product teardown posts, prompt and workflow examples, agent design notes, and before-and-after decision flows.
- Desired action: follow for build notes, ask technical questions, or test the product.

### Consultant, coach, or fractional executive

These readers help clients make better decisions. They care about repeatable methods and may become power users, affiliates, or design partners.

- Pain: client decisions stall because context is scattered and stakeholders talk past each other.
- What earns attention: facilitation methods, client-safe examples, packaged decision rituals, and ways to turn messy context into choices.
- Desired action: start a direct conversation about using the product with clients.

### Market-aware professional

These readers track AI, crypto, startups, or fast-moving markets. They need signal without performative certainty.

- Pain: feeds are full of confident takes that age badly.
- What earns attention: measured market reads, daily signal posts, and clear explanations of what changed and why it matters.
- Desired action: follow for signal, subscribe to updates, or try daily intelligence and market-read workflows.

## Content pillars

### 1. Decision discipline

Purpose: Teach the audience how to make better calls before introducing the product.

- Formats: one-page frameworks, "how I would decide" posts, decision checklists, mistakes to avoid, and retrospective threads.
- Example angles:
  - "A simple way to separate urgent decisions from loud ones."
  - "The question I ask before taking advice from an expert."
  - "How to write a decision brief when you only have 20 minutes."
- Primary CTA: "Save this for your next hard call" or "Comment with the decision you are stuck on."
- Success signal: saves, thoughtful comments, and replies with real decision problems.

### 2. AI advisors in practice

Purpose: Show how a council of AI advisors works in concrete situations.

- Formats: product walkthroughs, anonymized council sessions, role-by-role advisor breakdowns, prompt patterns, and screenshots.
- Example angles:
  - "I asked four advisors the same product question. The useful part was the disagreement."
  - "What changes when an AI advisor has a role, not just a prompt."
  - "A decision flow for founders who are tired of blank chat windows."
- Primary CTA: "Try this with your own question" or "Ask me for the template."
- Success signal: profile clicks, direct messages, signup intent, and technical questions.

### 3. Daily signal

Purpose: Make the account useful on a routine basis and connect the brand to sharp daily judgment.

- Formats: short daily briefs, market observations, founder prompts, trend notes, and "what to watch" posts.
- Example angles:
  - "Today the useful question is not what changed. It is what you can ignore."
  - "One AI signal worth watching this week."
  - "A founder prompt for Friday: what decision are you carrying into the weekend?"
- Primary CTA: "Follow for the next signal" or "Reply if you want the full brief."
- Success signal: repeat engagement from the same high-fit people and growth in followers who match target roles.

### 4. Build in public

Purpose: Turn product progress into trust. The tone should be candid, specific, and useful to other builders.

- Formats: build logs, metric notes, launch lessons, UX decisions, pricing tests, failed experiments, and roadmap tradeoffs.
- Example angles:
  - "We changed this onboarding step because users were skipping the question that mattered."
  - "The hard part of AI advisors is not the model. It is the shape of the conversation."
  - "What we learned from the first 20 decision sessions."
- Primary CTA: "Tell me how you would solve this" or "Join the early user group."
- Success signal: comments from builders and operators, shares into founder circles, and early user requests.

### 5. Customer and use-case stories

Purpose: Make the product outcome easy to picture without overclaiming.

- Formats: anonymized scenarios, before-and-after narratives, objection handling, and role-specific use cases.
- Example angles:
  - "A solo founder deciding whether to hire, automate, or cut scope."
  - "A consultant turning client chaos into three options."
  - "A builder using the council to pressure-test a launch plan."
- Primary CTA: "If this is your situation, send the decision and I will suggest a starting frame."
- Success signal: direct messages, demos, replies with similar stories, and product trials.

## Success criteria

### Activity targets

- Publish 3 to 5 LinkedIn posts per week.
- Spend at least as much time in comments and direct replies as in publishing.
- Review post performance weekly and update the content backlog with what worked.
- Repurpose only posts that produced a clear signal, such as saves, qualified comments, direct replies, or product actions.

### Leading indicators

- High-fit comments from founders, operators, consultants, builders, or market-aware professionals.
- Saves on frameworks and decision posts.
- Direct messages that mention a specific problem, not just "interesting product."
- Profile visits and website clicks after product or use-case posts.
- Repeat engagement from the same target accounts.

### Outcome metrics

- Qualified conversations started from LinkedIn.
- Product signups, demo requests, waitlist joins, or trial starts attributed to LinkedIn.
- Conversion from LinkedIn-sourced conversations into active product use.
- Audience quality, measured by target-role followers rather than raw follower count.
- Message-market fit, measured by repeated use of the same pain language in comments, calls, and replies.

### Quality bar

A post is successful when it changes the state of the relationship with the audience. Raw impressions help, but they are not enough. The operation should prefer a post that creates five useful conversations over one that earns broad passive reach.

## Content mix

Use this as the default weekly mix until performance data says otherwise:

- 2 decision discipline posts.
- 1 AI advisors in practice post.
- 1 daily signal or market-read post.
- 1 build-in-public or use-case story post.

If a post format produces qualified conversations twice in a row, repeat the structure with a new example before inventing a new format.

## Guardrails

- Do not claim the product replaces human judgment.
- Do not use vague AI hype. Name the decision, the constraint, and the tradeoff.
- Do not post market certainty for the sake of sounding decisive.
- Do not chase engagement from audiences that cannot buy, influence, or teach the product team something useful.
- Do not overproduce. A clear field note beats a polished generic post.

## Review cadence

Run a weekly review with four questions:

1. Which post created the most qualified conversations?
2. Which hook or example made people describe their own problem?
3. Which audience segment showed the strongest buying or design-partner signal?
4. What should be repeated, retired, or rewritten next week?

Record the answers in the content backlog so the operation improves from evidence, not mood.
